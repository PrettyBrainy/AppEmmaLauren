Waste Not Want Not

Let's Learn! Information to Get Started

Food waste does just what it says in the name: waste our world’s natural resources! Food we all throw away uses up 19% of our fertilizer, 21% of our freshwater, 21% of our landfills, and 18% of the land we grow fruits and vegetables on. Every year, our country spends about $220 billion growing, harvesting, and transporting 70 million tons of food that goes to waste. That’s a lot of money, and the land that goes into producing all of our food could cover ¾ of California!


All of the food we throw away also produces a lot of methane while decomposing. Methane is a powerful greenhouse gas that has 21 times the ability to trap heat underneath the layers of our atmosphere of carbon dioxide. All the food we grow, eat, and throw away contributes to 1/3 of human-caused greenhouse gas emissions. Even though it sounds a little crazy, food is one of the top contributors to global warming!

Take the Pledge Waste Not Want Not

The information above can help you understand the effects that the food you throw away can have on your wallet and the environment. Lead your family in a discussion about the changes that you will make in your home to waste less food. Pick a strategy that works best for you and your family to make your home more sustainable and free of food waste!. The information below can help guide you and your family on your journey.


To start, plan your meals ahead of time. Don’t buy anything that you aren’t going to eat or that you won’t use. Think about how much of the things on your shopping list you need to avoid buying more of than you are going to eat. Having food go bad is more of a pressing issue with fruit, vegetables, eggs, and other perishable goods. Over half of the fruits and vegetables in our country are wasted. You can help to reduce this number by canning fruits and vegetables that are about to go bad, storing fruits by themselves so they ripen more slowly, and only buying perishable goods only in the amounts you need.


You should also try to use up what’s in your fridge before going out to buy new ingredients. Try mixing new ingredients and find a new favorite dish! If you have food you know will go to waste in your home that is fresh or a nonperishable good, donate it to your local food rescue or food bank (Colorado Food Pantry Network website). If you have food that is expired or rotting, try starting a compost pile at your home to give all of those nutrients back to the soil. Go to Good Housekeeping’s guide for first-time composters if you want to learn how to compost. If you don’t want the responsibility and commitment of having a compost pile at home, send it to a local composting company like Compost Queens or Common Good Good Compost LLC. Have fun, and waste less!
