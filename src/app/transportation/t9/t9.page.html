New Closet, No Carbon

Let's Learn! Information to Get Started

Fast fashion stresses our natural resources and is destroying the planet. Fast fashion is the rapid factory production of garments, the styles of which have been stolen from legitimate designers. H&M is an example of fast fashion. The prices are cheap, but the costs to people and the planet are greater than we collectively can bear.


In addition, writes Tatiana Schlossberg in the New York Times, “More than 60% of fabric fibers are now synthetics, derived from fossil fuels, so if and when our clothing ends up in a landfill . . . it will not decay.” Incidentally, 85% of clothes in the U.S. do end up in landfills, further worsening their environmental impact by contributing to methane emissions. Let’s look at several ways fast fashion harms people and the planet.


Carbon dioxide emissions. Worldwide, the fashion industry “emits 1.7 billion tons of carbon dioxide per year — more than the amount produced by international flights and shipping” together, reports WBUR’s “Here & Now.”


Water. The water needed to grow the cotton for one pair of your favorite jeans is 680 to 1,500 gallons — for one pair of jeans. To grow just 8 ounces of cotton takes several gallons of water! Globally, the fast fashion industry is the second largest consumer of water. Among the top cotton producing nations, China and India are faced with water shortages such that they must choose between providing people clean drinking water or producing cotton, which makes money.


Toxins. When producing fast fashion, a plethora of toxic chemicals are used. These include cadmium, mercury, and lead, which can poison our water supply when you wash your clothes. Water isn’t the only thing being affected by these chemicals: don’t forget that we wear them next to our largest organ — our skin!


Landfills. On top of purchasing new clothes, the clothes you throw away negatively impact our environment. It is estimated that 12.8 million tons of clothing waste sits in landfills for hundreds of years, emitting harmful chemicals and dyes that contaminate the groundwater and the soil. This volume of clothing would fill a football field 14 times over.


Human Beings. When you buy fast fashion, you trap an entire generation of women in poverty. Women, most between the ages of 18 and 24, work the factories where 80% of fast fashion is made. These young women, whose birthright, like yours and mine, is safety, health, and education, earn less than $3 per day. Many work 14-hour days in unsafe and inhuman conditions. Abuse is common. To learn more, visit Remake.

Take the Pledge New Closet, No Carbon

Talk to your family and friends about the negative effects of fast fashion. Educate them on how you can save money and the environment and improve the lives of vulnerable women while still staying fashionable. How? Choose ethical, sustainable fashion and thrift. Instead of throwing away your clothes, donate them to your local thrift store and think twice about the repercussions of fast fashion. Whenever you and your family shop for new clothes, always search thrift stores first. If you need to buy new, check out our list of sustainable clothing brands in our resources tab.


Did you know that new, authentic designer wear often is available at thrift stores — at thrift store prices? On top of helping people get clothing in an affordable and environmentally sustainable way, many thrift stores often have goals of their own for making the community a better place. Look for local thrift stores and other types of secondhand stores in your area!
